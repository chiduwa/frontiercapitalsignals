Malawi's Department of Mines and Minerals Development has opened a first round of competitive licensing for lithium exploration rights covering six target zones, including the Salima and Kasungu deposits where geological surveys have identified indicated resources of approximately 2.3 million tonnes of lithium carbonate equivalent. Applications close in 120 days. License terms include a 5-year exploration period, a minimum work commitment of $4 million, and a government carried interest of 15% at the development stage.

Malawi has historically been one of the most undercapitalized mineral jurisdictions in Southern Africa despite a known resource endowment that includes uranium, rare earths, and now a quantified lithium footprint. The first-mover context is significant: lithium prices have rebounded sharply as battery supply chain diversification strategies push buying nations to fund exploration outside of established incumbents like Zimbabwe and the DRC. The government's intent to establish a battery materials value chain gives this concession round explicit policy backing.

The 120-day window is tight. Investors with interests in critical minerals and battery supply chains should begin technical review of the geological reports immediately, as these are publicly available through the Department of Mines. The $4 million work commitment is comparatively modest for the scale of potential resource, and Malawi's political stability and functioning legal system represent structural advantages over peer jurisdictions. This is a high-risk, high-upside early position.
